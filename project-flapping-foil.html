Abstract

Fish that swim in schools make rapid decisions: when to join, where to position themselves, and how to respond to their neighbors' movement. These decisions are thought to be driven by a combination of visual and hydrodynamic cues. However, ocean acidification — caused by rising atmospheric CO₂ dissolving into seawater — is known to impair sensory processing and behavior in fish. Whether elevated CO₂ disrupts the decision-making that underlies collective schooling is poorly understood.

This summer, I will investigate how CO₂-exposed silversides (Menidia sp.) make schooling decisions in a controlled flow tank setting. Rather than using live schools — which introduce uncontrolled variability — we use flapping foils that mimic the hydrodynamic signatures of schoolmates, creating a stable, repeatable artificial school. This allows us to isolate and observe the behavior of a single focal fish as it navigates and responds to its simulated neighbors.

I will run flow tank trials comparing the positioning and movement decisions of fish under elevated CO₂ to those under normal conditions. High-speed video will be used to record behavior throughout each trial. Results will improve our understanding of how ocean acidification disrupts the sensory and behavioral mechanisms that allow fish to school effectively.

As CO₂ levels continue to rise, determining whether collective behavior in fish is vulnerable to climate change is critical for predicting the ecological consequences for marine ecosystems that depend on schooling species.

Analysis Pipeline

To support this research, I built a MATLAB toolkit for extracting swimming kinematics from high-speed video tracking data. Key contributions include:

  • Midline alignment — implemented a least-squares rotation algorithm that re-orients each frame so the fish's swimming axis is parallel to X and the snout sits at the origin, enabling frame-by-frame comparison regardless of heading
  • FFT interpolation — upsampled sparse midlines (5–10 tracked landmarks) to 200 continuous body positions per frame using zero-padded FFT, producing smooth waveform profiles without landmark-spacing artifacts
  • Tail-beat frequency — extracted dominant beat frequency at the head and tail independently via temporal FFT of position time series
  • Amplitude envelope — computed mean lateral half-amplitude at each body position, yielding a full head-to-tail amplitude profile and summary metrics (head/tail ratio, min/max locations)
  • Propulsive wavelength — estimated from a spatial FFT of the amplitude profile
  • Body curvature — computed three-point geometric curvature at each of the 200 body positions and averaged across frames
  • 3-D support — extended the pipeline to handle Z-coordinate data, enabling dorso-ventral amplitude and curvature analysis for fin motion capture
  • GUI wrapper — built an interactive MATLAB app to make the pipeline accessible without editing code directly

Personal Statement

As an electrical engineering student, I am drawn to biological systems that behave like dynamic, signal-driven networks. This intersection of engineering and biology motivates my work on the Flapping Foil Experiment in Dr. Valentina Di Santo's lab at UC San Diego, where I study how rising CO₂ levels affect the schooling behavior of silversides. The opportunity to apply my technical skills to a question with real ecological consequences is exactly the kind of research I have been looking for.

My academic preparation has equipped me well for this work. I have maintained a 4.0 GPA while taking courses that directly support my research, including ECE 45, which focuses on signal processing and data analysis, and ECE 109, which I am currently taking to deepen my statistical analysis skills. These courses have given me the mathematical foundation to treat biological motion as a measurable, structured signal rather than noise.

That foundation has already shaped the tools I have built in this project. I developed an analysis pipeline that takes high-speed video tracking points along a fish's midline, converts the data into arrays, reconstructs a waveform of the fish's motion, and extracts kinematic metrics — all within an accessible interface. A previous project, a sensorized glove that detects hand signals from sensor data, introduced me to the challenges of extracting meaningful structure from noisy signals and iterating on a design until it works reliably.

This summer, I will expand that work by running flow tank trials comparing the schooling decisions of CO₂-exposed silversides to those under normal conditions. A key challenge in studying schooling behavior is isolating the decisions of a single fish from the chaos of a live group. We address this by using flapping foils to simulate the hydrodynamic signatures of schoolmates, creating a controlled and repeatable artificial school. I am also working to extend the analysis pipeline to three dimensions, which will allow us to capture fin motion and detect additional behavioral effects of CO₂.

My long-term goal is to use engineering to address climate change, particularly through bio-inspired robotics and intelligent systems. This research is preparing me for that path in concrete ways: I am strengthening my ability to design experiments, debug complex pipelines, and think critically when results do not behave as expected. With this foundation, I plan to pursue graduate study and eventually conduct independent research that bridges engineering and ecology.
