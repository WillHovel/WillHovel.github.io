Interactive Analysis Tool

This is a browser port of the MATLAB kinematics toolkit I built for the Di Santo Lab. Upload a CSV of digitized fish body landmarks, set your parameters, and the pipeline will align the midlines, run FFT interpolation, and extract kinematic metrics directly in your browser.

CSV format: Two formats are supported. DLC-style indexed columns (Fish1_P1_x, Fish1_P1_y) or named landmark columns (snout_X, snout_Y). Z columns are optional (3D mode). One row per frame.

Full Research Detail

Abstract

Fish that swim in schools make rapid decisions: when to join, where to position themselves, and how to respond to their neighbors' movement. These decisions are thought to be driven by a combination of visual and hydrodynamic cues. However, ocean acidification, caused by rising atmospheric CO2 dissolving into seawater, is known to impair sensory processing and behavior in fish. Whether elevated CO2 disrupts the decision-making that underlies collective schooling is poorly understood.

This summer, I will investigate how CO2-exposed silversides (Menidia sp.) make schooling decisions in a controlled flow tank setting. Rather than using live schools, which introduce uncontrolled variability, we use flapping foils that mimic the hydrodynamic signatures of schoolmates, creating a stable, repeatable artificial school. This allows us to isolate and observe the behavior of a single focal fish as it navigates and responds to its simulated neighbors.

Results will improve our understanding of how ocean acidification disrupts the sensory and behavioral mechanisms that allow fish to school effectively. As CO2 levels continue to rise, determining whether collective behavior in fish is vulnerable to climate change is critical for predicting ecological consequences for marine ecosystems that depend on schooling species.

Analysis Pipeline

To support this research, I built a MATLAB toolkit for extracting swimming kinematics from high-speed video tracking data. Key contributions include:

  • Midline alignment — implemented a least-squares rotation algorithm that re-orients each frame so the fish's swimming axis is parallel to X and the snout sits at the origin, enabling frame-by-frame comparison regardless of heading
  • FFT interpolation — upsampled sparse midlines (5 to 10 tracked landmarks) to 200 continuous body positions per frame using zero-padded FFT, producing smooth waveform profiles without landmark-spacing artifacts
  • Tail-beat frequency — extracted dominant beat frequency at the head and tail independently via temporal FFT of position time series
  • Amplitude envelope — computed mean lateral half-amplitude at each body position, yielding a full head-to-tail amplitude profile and summary metrics (head/tail ratio, min/max locations)
  • Propulsive wavelength — estimated from a spatial FFT of the amplitude profile
  • Body curvature — computed three-point geometric curvature at each of the 200 body positions and averaged across frames
  • 3-D support — extended the pipeline to handle Z-coordinate data, enabling dorso-ventral amplitude and curvature analysis for fin motion capture
  • Interactive GUI — built an interactive MATLAB app (and the browser tool above) so any researcher can analyze their CSV without editing code directly

Personal Statement

I am drawn to systems that are hard to measure. Whether it is a fish swimming in a flow tank, a hand making a gesture, or a robot reacting to a track, the interesting engineering problems all share the same structure: you have a physical system generating a messy, continuous signal, and you want to extract something meaningful and repeatable from it.

My background in electrical engineering has given me the mathematical foundation to approach these problems rigorously. Coursework in signal processing (ECE 45) and statistics (ECE 109) taught me to think carefully about noise, sampling, and what it actually means for an algorithm to generalize. That foundation shapes every project I work on. I do not just want something that works in demo conditions. I want to understand why it works, and what would make it fail.

What connects these projects is iteration. None of them worked on the first try. Each required diagnosing what was actually failing and fixing it at the right level of abstraction. That process of building, debugging, and redesigning is where I have learned the most, and it is what I want to keep doing.
