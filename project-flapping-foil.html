What Gets Fed Into the Tool

High-speed video of a swimming fish is recorded in a flow tank. Custom tracking software (DeepLabCut or manual) marks anatomical landmarks on every frame, producing XY (or XYZ) coordinate time-series. These spreadsheets are the raw input to the kinematics pipeline.

Abstract

Fish that swim in schools make rapid decisions: when to join, where to position themselves, and how to respond to their neighbors' movement. These decisions are driven by visual and hydrodynamic cues. Ocean acidification, caused by rising atmospheric CO2 dissolving into seawater, is known to impair sensory processing in fish. Whether elevated CO2 disrupts the decision-making underlying collective schooling is poorly understood.

I will investigate how CO2-exposed silversides (Menidia sp.) make schooling decisions in a controlled flow tank setting. Rather than using live schools, we use flapping foils that mimic the hydrodynamic signatures of schoolmates, creating a repeatable artificial school. This allows us to isolate the behavior of a single focal fish responding to its simulated neighbors.

Analysis Pipeline

  • Midline alignment — least-squares rotation re-orients each frame so the swimming axis is parallel to X and the snout sits at origin, enabling frame-by-frame comparison regardless of heading
  • FFT interpolation — upsamples sparse midlines (5 to 10 tracked landmarks) to 200 continuous body positions per frame using zero-padded FFT, producing smooth waveform profiles without landmark-spacing artifacts
  • Tail-beat frequency — dominant beat frequency extracted at head and tail independently via temporal FFT of position time series
  • Amplitude envelope — mean lateral half-amplitude at each body position, yielding a full head-to-tail profile and summary metrics
  • Propulsive wavelength — estimated from a spatial FFT of the amplitude profile
  • Body curvature — three-point geometric curvature at each of the 200 body positions, averaged across frames
  • Fin/tail vector analysis — for any pair of named landmarks, computes pitch, yaw, roll angles, tip trajectory, cumulative tip distance, and angular velocity; animated with a live 3D canvas

Personal Statement

I am drawn to systems that are hard to measure. Whether it is a fish swimming in a flow tank, a hand making a gesture, or a robot reacting to a track, the interesting engineering problems share the same structure: a physical system generating a messy, continuous signal, and the goal of extracting something meaningful and repeatable from it.

What connects these projects is iteration. None of them worked on the first try. Each required diagnosing what was actually failing and fixing it at the right level of abstraction. That process of building, debugging, and redesigning is where I have learned the most.
