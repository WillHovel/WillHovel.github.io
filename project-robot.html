Project Overview

A line-following robot built for the ECE 5 Lab 4 competition. The robot uses an array of photoresistors to detect a black line on a white surface and a PID control loop to keep itself centered on the line, correcting its motor speeds in real time based on where it detects the line relative to where it should be.

The team placed first overall across two of three events, winning the drag race (pure speed) and the frequency sweep (handling a track with increasingly tight turns), while placing second in the circuit loop (sustained laps).

Final robot — custom lightshield keeps ambient light off the photoresistor array

PID Control System

The error is computed as the actual line position minus the desired center position. The PID controller uses three terms to compute motor corrections:

P
Proportional
Corrections based on current error magnitude
I
Integral
Corrects steady-state error by accumulating past errors
D
Derivative
Predicts future error from rate of change, damping oscillations
PID control loop: error = actual line position minus desired position, fed through P, I, and D terms to set motor speeds

Tuning Strategy

Each event required different PID parameters and speeds, because the optimal tradeoff between speed and correction accuracy changed with track geometry.

  • Drag Race — maximum speed (nominal 200, potentiometer 60). P=40, I=100, D=100. Prioritized raw speed with minor corrections since the track had few sharp turns.
  • Frequency Sweep — slower speed (nominal 30, potentiometer 100) with P=100, I=100, D=100. The track had increasingly tight curves requiring aggressive, accurate corrections at the expense of speed.
  • Circuit Loop — balanced approach (nominal 150, potentiometer 70), P=95, I=100, D=100. Smooth, fast laps requiring both accuracy and sustained speed.

Hardware Modifications

Updated chassis CAD with lightshield — blocked ambient light from the photoresistor array to reduce detection noise

The team modified the base chassis CAD to improve reliability. A custom lightshield was designed to block ambient light from the photoresistors, reducing noise in the line-detection signal. Late in the competition, the team identified that the ball wheel was inconsistently making contact with the track, alternately rolling and lifting, which introduced unpredictable speed changes. The fix was to tape the ball wheel in a fixed position, ensuring consistent ground contact and predictable speed throughout each run. Wires were also added on top of the robot to keep the lightshield elevated and prevent it from dragging on the track.

Competition Notes

First place overall out of all ECE 5 Lab 4 teams

After the competition, the team reflected that allowing the ball wheel to make consistent contact with the track could have provided additional passive speed, a potential improvement for the drag race and circuit loop. The key insight was that predictability in the mechanical system is just as important as the control algorithm: a well-tuned PID controller still fails if the physical platform behaves inconsistently.
