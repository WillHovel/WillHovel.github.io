Project Overview

A line-following robot built for the ECE 5 Lab 4 competition. The robot uses an array of photoresistors to detect a black line on a white surface and a PID control loop to keep itself centered on the line, correcting motor speeds in real time based on where the line is detected relative to center.

The team placed first overall across two of three events, winning the drag race (pure speed) and the frequency sweep (handling increasingly tight turns), while placing second in the circuit loop (sustained laps).

PID Control System

Error is computed as the actual line position minus the desired center position. Three terms combine to correct motor speeds:

P
Proportional
Corrections based on current error magnitude
I
Integral
Corrects steady-state error by accumulating past errors
D
Derivative
Predicts future error from rate of change, damping oscillation

Tuning Strategy

Each event required different PID parameters because the optimal tradeoff between speed and correction accuracy changed with track geometry.

  • Drag Race — maximum speed (nominal 200, pot 60). P=40, I=100, D=100. Prioritized raw speed with minor corrections; track had few sharp turns.
  • Frequency Sweep — slower speed (nominal 30, pot 100) with P=100, I=100, D=100. Increasingly tight curves required aggressive, accurate corrections at the expense of speed.
  • Circuit Loop — balanced (nominal 150, pot 70), P=95, I=100, D=100. Smooth, fast laps requiring both accuracy and sustained speed.

Hardware Modifications

Final robot — custom lightshield keeps ambient light off the photoresistor array

The team modified the base chassis CAD to improve reliability. A custom lightshield was designed to block ambient light from the photoresistors, reducing noise in the line-detection signal. Late in the competition, the team identified that the ball wheel was inconsistently making contact with the track, alternately rolling and lifting, introducing unpredictable speed changes. Taping the ball wheel in a fixed position ensured consistent ground contact throughout each run.

Competition Notes

The key insight was that predictability in the mechanical system is just as important as the control algorithm: a well-tuned PID controller still fails if the physical platform behaves inconsistently.
