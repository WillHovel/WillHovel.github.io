Personal Statement

I'm drawn to systems that are hard to measure. Whether it's a fish swimming in a flow tank, a hand making a gesture, or a robot reacting to a track, the interesting engineering problems all share the same structure: you have a physical system generating a messy, continuous signal, and you want to extract something meaningful and repeatable from it.

My background in electrical engineering has given me the mathematical foundation to approach these problems rigorously. Coursework in signal processing (ECE 45) and statistics (ECE 109) taught me to think carefully about noise, sampling, and what it actually means for an algorithm to generalize. That foundation shapes every project I work on — I don't just want something that works in demo conditions. I want to understand why it works, and what would make it fail.

The projects on this site reflect that. The fish kinematics pipeline started as a data cleaning problem and turned into a full signal processing system — midline alignment, FFT interpolation, frequency analysis, curvature estimation. The sensorized glove required designing a PCB, routing USB differential pairs, reading noisy analog signals, and then figuring out a classification approach that was actually reliable. The line-following robot was an exercise in the feedback loop between algorithm and physical system: even a well-tuned PID controller fails if the mechanical platform behaves inconsistently.

What connects these projects is iteration. None of them worked on the first try. Each required diagnosing what was actually failing — whether that was a grounding issue in the PCB, a numerical artifact in the FFT, or a ball wheel that lifted off the track at exactly the wrong moment — and fixing it at the right level of abstraction. That process of building, debugging, and redesigning is where I've learned the most, and it's what I want to keep doing.

Long-term, I want to apply engineering to problems that sit at the boundary between disciplines — bio-inspired robotics, intelligent systems for environmental sensing, tools that make complex data legible to researchers who aren't engineers. I plan to pursue graduate study and eventually conduct independent research. The work I've done so far has shown me that the most interesting problems are usually the ones nobody has built the right tool for yet.
